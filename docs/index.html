Population
Mountains

By Matt Daniels

Megacities visualized using population data for every 1,000 square meters.

This is a story about how to perceive the population size of cities.

Here is New York City, a region of 18 million people. Each dot represents 1,000 square meters. Deeper shades of red represent more people.

But there’s another way to look at NYC’s population. Let's grow each dot into a 3D block.

The taller the block, the more people. NYC's population now resembles a mountainous terrain.

Zoom out and the world looks different than you might expect—a new way to see where we live.

When I saw cites from my US-centric perspective, it was eye-opening.

New York wasn’t just 18 million people—it had a form. Every city was a unique mountain that might be like the steep peaks of lower Manhattan or the sprawling hills of suburban Atlanta. When I first saw a city in 3D, I had a feel for its size that didn’t exist before.

That feeling goes a long way to improve my own geographic instincts. In 1993, there were 14 cities over 10 million people. Today, 20 more cities qualify (and an additional 11 by 2030), with many sprouting from farmland in our lifetimes. Unless you’ve visited or tracked its growth closely, the scale of a new, 10 million- person city can be hard to believe.

Among them is Kinshasa, DR Congo (13 million people), which I now know to be bigger than historically familiar cities. I had seen it outrank Paris (11 million people) in population bar charts and data tables. Yet it wasn’t until I saw the population as a form, rather than a number, could I appreciate how large Kinshasa had become.
